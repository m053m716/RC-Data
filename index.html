Working Title

*Task-Related Neural Activity Following Primary Motor Cortical Ischemia in Rats

Authors

Andrea Pack**, Max Murphy**, Scott Barbay, Randolph Nudo, & David Guggenmos

Notes

*Work-in-progress

**Equal first-author contribution

Abstract

Background

Acquired injuries to primary motor cortex (M1) contribute to motor impairment and disability. Functional recovery is predicated on the reorganization of spared areas, which has been demonstrated through post-injury plasticity in cortical motor map representations and neuroanatomical projection and termination patterns.

Objective

The purpose of this study was to understand how neurophysiological outputs of spared motor areas relate to motor recovery of a skilled reach task following an ischemic infarct to M1.

Method

We examined changes in extracellularly-recorded single-unit activity within ipsilesional pre-motor (PM) and contralesional M1 cortices of rats during a behavioral task after a unilateral ischemic injury to ipsilesional M1. Details.

Result

These data show a shift in neuronal firing patterns in the contralateral PM and ipsilateral M1 during behavioral recovery in injured rats compared to a non-injured control group, suggesting that spike-timing properties are altered in specific phases of the task, and that this altered activity supports the ability to re-acquire functional motor behaviors after stroke. Details.

Overview

Results

Reach duration decreases as rats recover.

Single-Channel Multi-Unit Spiking Dynamics demonstrate weaker trends.

Changes in Population Dynamics are most-evident during post-op days 8-15.

Control system dynamics are restored by 3-4 weeks after injury.

Discussion

Multi-unit spiking does not differentiate ischemic and intact rats at the level of single-channel spiking activity.
